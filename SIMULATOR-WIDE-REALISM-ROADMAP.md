# Simulator-Wide Realism Roadmap

This is a future roadmap, separate from the completed strategy, voting, and
alliance overhaul.

## Main conclusion

The voting engine is no longer the simulator's main realism limitation.

The next major improvement should be how contestants experience the game
between votes: what they know, what they want, who they trust, where they can
have conversations, how they learn, and what the audience is allowed to see.

Do not prioritize more vote rules next. Prioritize human cognition, limited
information, persistent goals, multidimensional relationships, and editing.

## Highest-value missing systems

### 1. Personal knowledge and information networks

Every strategic fact should have contestant-specific knowledge:

- Who knows it?
- Who told them?
- Do they believe it?
- Is it current, incomplete, exaggerated, or false?
- Do they know that another contestant also knows?

This should cover idols, advantages, targets, alliances, betrayals,
relationships, challenge throws, and vote pitches.

This would create natural misunderstandings without relying on random chaos.

### 2. Persistent intentions and personal strategy

Contestants should carry plans across episodes:

- Intended final three
- Preferred shields
- Endgame goats
- Backup allies
- Pre-merge targets
- Revenge goals
- Jury-management plans
- Advantage plans
- Conditions that would justify betraying an ally

Plans should evolve when circumstances change instead of being rebuilt from
scratch every episode.

### 3. Multidimensional relationships

A single bond value cannot represent every human relationship.

Separate relationship dimensions should include:

- Affection
- Trust
- Strategic respect
- Fear
- Obligation
- Resentment
- Attraction

Two contestants could like each other personally while distrusting each other
strategically. Someone could resent an ally but still respect their ability.
This would make decisions such as “I love them, but I cannot face them at the
end” emerge naturally.

### 4. Camp time and physical conversation access

Not everyone should be able to speak with everyone before every Tribal.

Episodes should contain a limited social schedule:

- Morning work groups
- Camp chores
- Water-well conversations
- Reward groups
- Idol searches
- Private walks
- Challenge preparation
- Post-challenge scrambling
- Time before leaving for Tribal

Location and time should determine who can pitch, overhear, interrupt, verify
numbers, or notice suspicious behavior.

### 5. Adaptation and learning

Static stats should define starting tendencies. Experience should modify
behavior during the season.

Examples:

- A failed lie makes someone more cautious.
- Being blindsided makes someone verify numbers.
- A successful move increases confidence.
- Repeated rejection creates desperation or withdrawal.
- Watching an idol play changes future split-vote behavior.
- Jury feedback changes endgame strategy.
- A failed negotiation changes who someone approaches next time.

### 6. Production and editing layer

Simulation truth and viewer truth should be separate systems.

The game determines what happened. The edit determines what the audience sees:

- Screen-time distribution
- Confessional distribution
- Winner edit
- Decoy winner
- Invisible player
- Villain edit
- Growth arc
- Comic-relief edit
- Unreliable narrator
- Flashback revealing a hidden plan after Tribal

This may be the single biggest storytelling improvement. A realistic vote can
still feel predictable if every important fact is shown before Tribal.

### 7. Individual jury psychology

Each juror should have personal voting criteria:

- Loyalty
- Strategic control
- Honesty
- Challenge strength
- Social treatment
- Underdog respect
- Resentment
- Ownership of moves
- Respect for a particular playstyle

Jurors should remember how finalists treated them, how they were eliminated,
whether finalists owned their moves, and whether Final Tribal answers match
what the juror witnessed.

### 8. Dynamic social status

Contestants should occupy changing roles beyond formal alliances:

- Social center
- Information broker
- Camp provider
- Challenge leader
- Outsider
- Shield
- Goat
- Swing vote
- Trusted lieutenant
- Irritating but useful player
- Power couple

Status should affect who gets invited into conversations, whose pitch receives
attention, and whose suspicious absence from camp gets noticed.

### 9. Challenge continuity

Challenges should create consequences that persist:

- Fatigue
- Injury
- Fear
- Practice and improvement
- Role specialization
- Confidence
- Blame for specific mistakes
- Deliberate role assignment
- Believable challenge-throw coordination

A contestant should not simply be “weak.” They may excel at puzzles, panic
underwater, struggle while hungry, or improve after repeated practice.

### 10. Separate Total Drama and Survivor realism profiles

The shows overlap, but their storytelling priorities are different.

#### Survivor profile

- Scarcity
- Coalition math
- Information control
- Jury management
- Advantages
- Camp labor
- Social positioning

#### Total Drama profile

- Host interference
- Comedy
- Rivalries
- Public humiliation
- Rule changes
- Sabotage
- Challenge-specific story beats
- Personality conflict
- Production manipulation

A shared engine can support both, but event frequency, tone, and narrative
logic should be tuned separately.

## Visual improvements

The next visual systems should explain relationships and change over time
instead of adding more dense dashboards:

- Evolving relationship network
- “Who knows what” information map
- Alliance history timelines
- Contestant strategy pages showing shields, targets, and endgame intentions
- Episode timelines connecting camp events to Tribal
- Separate trust and affection indicators
- Jury sentiment timelines
- Screen-time and edit charts
- Camp-location diagrams for strategic conversations
- Visible uncertainty instead of exact hidden percentages on normal screens

## Recommended implementation order

1. Multidimensional relationships
2. Personal knowledge and information flow
3. Persistent intentions and endgame plans
4. Camp time and location simulation
5. Adaptation and learning
6. Production and editing layer
7. Individual jury psychology
8. Challenge continuity
9. Visual relationship and information maps
10. Separate Total Drama and Survivor tuning profiles

## Guiding principle

The simulator already has strong vote mechanics. The next realism leap should
come from believable people:

- People know different things.
- People misunderstand one another.
- People remember what happened.
- People pursue goals over time.
- People can like and distrust someone simultaneously.
- People cannot be everywhere at once.
- People learn from success and failure.
- The audience does not see the complete truth until the story is ready.
